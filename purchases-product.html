From mission to solution

Type

Long-term project

Role

UI/UX Designer

Timeline

Jan 2013–Mar 2018

Market

In Russia, when companies need services, they place their needs on special websites as auctions. Other companies, that can provide services, search for those auctions to participate in them and win the contract.

We call the first companies customers, and the second — suppliers.

Mission

Our product aggregates auctions from different sources and provides a search for suppliers. Along with the search, the product helps to organize and analyze potential auctions.

Competitors

  • a free government website
  • a number of paid websites with the same goal

We bet on a good search and usability. In design and implementation of every feature, we followed UX best practices. Our strategy was not based on first to market, but best to market. It paid of. The product is still growing and already has over 18k users.

User flow

User flow can be divided into 3 steps: search, analysis, and participation. On each step of the flow, we uncovered user problems and tried to solve them. Here's the user flow in our service.

Solution

Step 1. Search

After the research of how our users were searching on the government website, we revealed 3 key problems: a difficult search form, too many irrelevant results, and repetitive search. Our solution is to provide users with a better search without those problems.

Search form and results

Based on user testing and competitive research, the most important search fields are “keywords” and “region”.

Search results contain data essential for a decision to go deeper or not.

Advanced search

Only after the key scenarios did we pay attention to less frequent cases. Read about form's lifetime

Saved searches

With “saved searches”, users don't need to compose their requests every day.

With the subscription to “saved searches”, users don't need to even search—they can receive data automatically via emails.

Saving and subscribing

To connect “saved searches” and subscription transparently, we made a continuous process—one control to make it step by step.

UX testings showed that it was much easier to subscribe right after saving a search.

Analytics showed that the amount of subscribed users increased considerably.

Step 2. Analysis

On this step, users had 2 key problems. The first one is that auction data was too difficult to understand because of the sophisticated legal jargon and overlapping content. The second problem was that users couldn't check if they could trust customers.

Auction's data

We aggregated data from different sources and showed the most important using the same view to decrease the time needed for a decision.

Analyzing customer

We created an analytics feature that allows suppliers to check potential customer and to uncover an affiliation of participants. I designed a user flow where suppliers can analyze customers by their previous auctions: competitors, winners, competition, and price reduction.

Step 3. Preparation for participation

On this step, users were doing a lot of manual work: they copied data from the website by hand, created Excel files, and combined it all into emails for sharing with their coworkers. We tried to simplify this process for them.

Another problem users had was that customers often changed conditions of participation and there wasn't any way to learn about it. In such situations, suppliers didn't change their application documents and failed auctions.

Favorite auctions

We created an online storage with opportunities to mark auctions differently, write comments, and export to Excel.

Changes in auctions

To solve the problem about customer's updates, we learned to identify changes and notify suppliers about them.

Promo and info part of the website

Apart from helping our users search and work with auctions, we educate them and keep updated in this hard world of the Russian business.

Landing page

In the beginning the service was free. We were extending our clients base and getting a feedback. This page appeared after we reached MVP.
