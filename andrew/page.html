Web Legal Issues:
What you Should Know

There are 3 main categories of Web Legal Issues you should be aware of: Trademark Law, Linking and Framing, and Domain Name Law

Trademark Law

Trademarks are commonly confused with copyrights and patents. All three protect some form of intellectual property, but each corresponds to a distinct area. For instance, copyrights only apply to artistic or literary works, while patents are explicitly for inventions, Trademarks protect elements of a company brand identity. For example, distinct brand names and logos can be trademarked since they relate to a company image.

  • In order to be protectable, trademarks must be registered with the United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO).
  • Once registered, the owner has responsibility to monitor any unauthorized use of their mark.
  • If a case of infringement were to go unnoticed by the owner, it is possible that they could lose ownership of the mark.
  • The increase in internet accessibility has made this an easier task for trademark owners, and has correlated with an uptick in infringement allegations.
  • Leading the reader to conclude that your site is connected with or endorsed by a trademarked product or owner can lead to your liability.
  • Any link that falsely leads the end user to conclude that the web page author is affiliated, approved, or sponsored by the trademark owner could lead to a claim of trademark infringement [1].

Linking and Framing

Generally adding links to a website does not present any legal issues or obligations. The physical act of writing an HTML link does not convey any endorsement, authorship, or ownership [3].

  • No permission is required when providing the name or publicly accessible URL (Web address) for any website [2].
  • Third party pages and images should not be linked into the frame [1].
  • When viewing framed information, your computer is connected to the site doing the framing - NOT the site whose page appears in the frame [2].
  • Framing is generally unpopular with websites whose content is framed on another site (unless they have agreed to it).
  • Websites that frame the content of other sites are often seen as stealing the other site content [2].

Domain Name Law

When it comes to web law and domain names, most of the issues center around Domain Name Disputes. That is, who really has the right to own a certain domain name? Even with a properly registered site, you may find yourself on either side of a dispute, so it is a good idea to understand the basics

In order to dispute someone using a domain name, you must prove that:

  • the name is the same (or confusingly similar) to your registered name,
  • the other party has no legitimate business interest in it, and
  • that the domain name was registered and used in bad faith

Ways you can prove your right to a domain name is by showing:

  • the name is the same (or confusingly similar) to your registered name,
  • the other party has no legitimate business interest in it, and
  • that the domain name was registered and used in bad faith
  • the domain name was used prior to any notice of the dispute.
  • you have been commonly known by the second level domain name, or
  • you are making fair use of the domain name, without intent of commercial gain, misleadingly diverting consumers, or tarnishing the trademark at issue.
